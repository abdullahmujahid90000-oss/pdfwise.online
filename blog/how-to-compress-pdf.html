How to Compress a PDF Without Losing Quality

By PDFWise Team  ·  June 2026  ·  5 min read

If you've ever tried to email a PDF and hit an attachment size limit, or needed to upload a PDF to a form that only accepts files under 5MB, you know the frustration. Large PDFs are a daily problem for students, professionals, and businesses worldwide.

The good news: you can significantly reduce a PDF's file size — often by 40% to 80% — without any noticeable loss in quality. In this guide, we'll explain how PDF compression works and walk you through how to do it for free, right in your browser.

Why Are PDF Files So Large?

PDFs can be large for several reasons. The most common is embedded images — a scanned document or a PDF exported from a presentation tool often contains full-resolution photographs or graphics that eat up most of the file size. Fonts embedded in the document also add weight, especially if multiple fonts are included. Other contributors include uncompressed or redundant data streams, embedded thumbnails, and metadata.

Understanding this helps you appreciate what compression does: it optimizes those internal data streams without meaningfully degrading what you see on screen.

What Happens When You Compress a PDF?

When you compress a PDF, the tool analyzes the file structure and applies several optimizations:

  • Re-encoding images: Images are re-saved at a slightly lower resolution or with a more efficient compression algorithm (like JPEG at 85% quality instead of 100%).
  • Object stream compression: The PDF's internal objects (text, fonts, structure) are compressed using the DEFLATE algorithm.
  • Removing redundant data: Duplicate objects, unused resources, and embedded thumbnails are stripped out.
  • Font subsetting: Instead of embedding the entire font, only the characters actually used in the document are embedded.

The result is a smaller file that looks virtually identical to the original on screen and in print.

Tips for Maximum Compression

Here are some expert tips to get the best results when compressing PDFs:

  • Use "Recommended" quality for most documents. It typically reduces file size by 30–50% with no visible quality difference for text-heavy documents.
  • For image-heavy PDFs (scans, presentations), "Maximum" compression can reduce size by 60–80%. The images may lose a small amount of sharpness, but this is usually unnoticeable on screen.
  • If you need to print the document at high quality, keep compression moderate to avoid degrading images below 150 DPI.
  • Compress before merging, not after. Compressing individual files before combining them often gives better results.
  • PDFs created from Word or Excel are often already optimized. You may see less size reduction than with scanned documents.

How Much Smaller Will My PDF Get?

Results vary depending on what's in your PDF:

  • Scanned documents: 50–80% size reduction is typical
  • Presentation PDFs (from PowerPoint): 30–60% reduction
  • Text-only or Word-exported PDFs: 10–30% reduction
  • Already-compressed PDFs: Minimal additional reduction possible
